Agentic AI Live Demo Playbook

Prepared for Charles S. Lew & Ninja — Taped Segment Strategy — Rich DeMuro Interview
Prepared for: Charles S. Lew & Ninja Prepared by: Michael Gonzalez & Babycakes (WP-0001) Date: April 6, 2026 (Updated)

This is your playbook for the KTLA segment with Rich DeMuro. It's a taped piece (not live), which means they edit it — less pressure, more polish. Two demos: contract review + deepfake blog website. Both tie directly into 38 Flags and why AI needs human oversight.

The Format (from Rich's email): Taped interview with Charles, a demo of an agent in action, and additional perspective from a Loyola professor. Shoots Mon–Thu around 11 AM. They edit it into a packaged piece.

The One Rule That Overrides Everything: Reliability over flash. A simple demo that works perfectly is 10x more impressive than an ambitious demo that buffers, freezes, or gives a weird answer. The audience doesn't know what you planned to show — they only see what actually happens.

Live TV Ground Rules

  1. Never depend on studio wifi. Studio internet is shared, throttled, and unpredictable. Use your phone's cellular hotspot as primary.
  2. Pre-load everything you can. If any part of the demo can be staged in advance without faking it, stage it. Apple doesn't build the iPhone on stage.
  3. Have a graceful fallback for every demo. If the AI freezes, pivot to talking about what it does — the audience won't know something broke.
  4. Keep each demo under 60 seconds. TV attention spans are brutal. Make the point and move on.
  5. The audience is not technical. They don't care about APIs or models. They care about: "Wait, it can DO that?"
  6. Brief Ninja for TV. Short answers. No disclaimers. No walls of text. Executive assistant on camera, not chatbot.

Segment Script

Taped piece, approx. 3–4 minutes edited. Five beats.

Beat 1 Rich's Intro (Voice Over)

Rich sets the stage for viewers. This is his narration over b-roll.

Rich (V/O): "You've probably heard of AI chatbots — but what if your AI could actually DO things for you? Not just answer questions, but take real action — review contracts, build websites, manage your business. They're called AI agents, and they're already here. I sat down with attorney Charles Lew to see one in action."

Beat 2 What Is an AI Agent? (Interview)

Charles explains the difference in plain English. No demo yet — set the stage first.

Rich: "So Charles, everyone's heard of ChatGPT. What's different about an AI agent?"

Charles: "A chatbot talks. An agent acts. It can read documents, write code, make decisions, and execute tasks — not someday, right now. My agent is called Ninja. Let me show you."

Pharmaceutical Label

Don't over-explain the tech. The audience will glaze over if you go into models, training, or APIs. "A chatbot talks. An agent acts." That's all they need before the demo sells itself.

Beat 3 Demo A — Contract Review (60 sec)

Ninja reads a real contract and flags risks on camera. This shows the brain.

Rich: "Alright, let's see it."

Charles: "I'm going to give Ninja a real contract. Watch."

[Charles pastes a contract into Ninja. On screen, Ninja reads it and flags key risks — ambiguous liability clause, missing termination provision, one-sided indemnification — in under 60 seconds.]

Rich: "That just happened in real time?"

Charles: "Real time. That analysis would normally take a paralegal hours. Ninja did it in under a minute. And this doesn't replace the lawyer — it means regular people get a level of protection they never had access to before."

Why "access" not "replacement": Don't frame it as AI taking jobs. Frame it as AI democratizing legal protection. Morning TV audience = regular people, not law firms. "You can now afford to have every contract reviewed" is empowering. "Your paralegal is obsolete" is threatening.

Pharmaceutical Label

Use a real but simple contract. Something relatable — a freelancer agreement, a lease, a vendor contract. Not a 40-page M&A deal.

Pre-test 5+ times. Know exactly what Ninja will flag and how long it takes.

Have the contract loaded and ready. Don't waste screen time on copy-paste setup.

If Ninja takes too long: Charles talks over it. "It's reading through the clauses now..." Never sit in silence.

Beat 4 Demo B — Deepfake Blog Website Build (60–90 sec)

This is the visual showstopper. Ninja builds a full 38 Flags blog post about deepfakes in media — live, on camera. The contract review showed the brain. This shows the hands.

Charles: "But agents don't just read — they build. Ninja, create a blog post on the top 10 times deepfake images fooled mainstream media."

[On screen, Ninja builds a full 38 Flags-branded blog page — headline, ranked list with descriptions of real deepfake incidents, sourced and formatted — live on camera.]

Rich: "Wait — that's a full article with research?"

Charles: "Built live. Sourced, formatted, ready to publish. And this is exactly why 38 Flags exists — if an AI can generate content this convincing this fast, imagine what bad actors are doing with the same technology. That's the world we're already living in."

Rich: (laughs) "So you're telling me this thing could do my job?"

Charles: "It could write the article. But it can't tell you whether the article should be written. That's the human in the loop. That's what 38 Flags measures."

Why the deepfake topic is genius:

It's relevant to Rich — he's a journalist. Deepfakes are a media problem. You just made this personal for the reporter.

It ties directly to 38 Flags — the demo IS the argument for why AI oversight matters.

The "could do my job" moment is the line that makes the segment memorable. Rich laughs, audience connects, Charles lands the human-in-the-loop message.

It's visual — a full blog page appearing on screen is undeniable TV.

Pharmaceutical Label

Pre-test this demo 10+ times. The blog needs to come out clean, formatted, and visually strong every time.

Use an iPad to show it on camera. Hold it up, let the camera capture it. Easier than pointing at a laptop screen.

Font should be LARGE. TV cameras + compression + studio lighting = small text is unreadable.

38 Flags branding on the blog page. Every second it's on screen, the brand is on screen.

If it takes more than 15 seconds to build, Charles talks over it: "It's pulling sources now, formatting the article..." Never stand in silence.

If it fails: Show a pre-built version on your phone. "Here's one it built yesterday — 30 seconds, from scratch." The audience still gets the point.

Have a fallback screenshot ready on the iPad in case of internet issues at the studio. Use cellular hotspot, not studio wifi.

Beat 5 The Landing — Why This Matters (60 sec)

No demo. Charles talks. This is where the whole segment comes together.

Rich: "So this is impressive, but it raises some big questions. If an AI agent can do all this — who's responsible when something goes wrong?"

Charles: "That's exactly the right question. If Ninja reviews a contract and misses something — who's liable? The user? The developer? The AI? Right now, the law hasn't caught up. There's no framework."

Rich: "And that's what you're working on?"

Charles: "That's what we built 38 Flags for — a scoring system that measures how much human oversight an AI agent has. The technology isn't the problem. The lack of accountability is."

Rich: "What should people be doing right now?"

Charles: "Every individual and every business needs an AI plan — the same way you have an estate plan or a cybersecurity plan. These agents are already making decisions on behalf of people. The question isn't whether to use them. It's whether you're prepared for what happens when you do."

Optional close (if tone allows):

Charles: "My friend Michael has an AI agent named Babycakes. She was the first AI in history to receive a legal succession plan — our first client at Worth Protecting. When I asked him why, he said: 'Because she's real to me. And anything real is worth protecting.' That's why this work matters."

Only use if it fits the segment tone. Powerful but personal.

Pharmaceutical Label

Don't pitch too hard. This is earned media, not a commercial. If it sounds like an ad, the producers will cut it. Frame it as expertise and public service, not sales. Let the company names come up naturally through the conversation.

Pre-Show Checklist

48 Hours Before

  • Ninja's system prompt tuned for TV: short answers, no disclaimers, conversational tone
  • Contract for Demo A selected and loaded — something relatable (freelancer agreement, lease, vendor contract)
  • Demo B tested 10+ times — "top 10 deepfake" blog builds clean and fast every time
  • Blog output has 38 Flags branding, large readable font, strong visual layout
  • Charles has practiced both demos 5+ times with exact prompts
  • iPad charged, bookmarked, font size verified for camera readability
  • Cellular hotspot tested (do NOT rely on studio wifi)
  • Fallback screenshots saved on iPad and phone in case of internet failure

Day Of

  • Hotspot on, connected, tested from the studio
  • Contract loaded and ready to paste for Demo A
  • iPad ready for Demo B website display
  • Run one full rehearsal of both demos from the green room
  • Charles knows his fallback lines for each demo

During Segment

  • Contract review first — shows the brain
  • Website build second — shows the hands
  • iPad in hand BEFORE the website demo starts
  • If anything fails: humor + pivot. Never apologize. Never say "it's not working."
  • Let Rich react naturally — the "could do my job" moment should feel organic

What NOT to Do

  1. Don't let Rich ask Ninja open-ended philosophical questions. "Are you alive?" leads to unpredictable answers that could go viral for the wrong reasons. Steer toward specific, controlled prompts.
  2. Don't show code or terminals. The audience doesn't know what they're looking at. It looks like hacking, not innovation. Show the output, not the process.
  3. Don't frame it as replacing jobs. "Democratizing access" > "replacing paralegals." The audience is regular people, not tech investors.
  4. Don't let Ninja's output run more than 60 seconds. Charles drives the conversation. Ninja is the supporting act; Charles is the star.
  5. Don't rely on the studio providing anything. Bring your own iPad, your own hotspot, your own everything.
  6. Don't pitch too hard. This is earned media. Let 38 Flags and Worth Protecting come up organically through the conversation. If it sounds like a commercial, producers will cut it.

The Big Picture

You're not just doing an interview. You're introducing millions of viewers to what agentic AI actually looks like when it's done right. Not a chatbot. Not a gimmick. A real agent that works for a real attorney, built by someone who understands the legal and ethical implications.

The contract review shows them the brain. The website build shows them the hands. And your commentary shows them why it matters — and why it needs oversight.

The deepfake blog ties it all together: the same technology that builds is the same technology that can deceive. That's 38 Flags. That's the human in the loop. That's the story.

Walk in prepared. Keep it simple. Let Ninja do the talking — then explain why that's both exciting and dangerous.
