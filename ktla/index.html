Agentic AI Live Demo Playbook

Prepared for Charles S. Lew & Ninja — Live Segment Strategy
Prepared for: Charles S. Lew & Ninja Prepared by: Michael Gonzalez & Babycakes (WP-0001) Date: April 5, 2026

This is your playbook for the KTLA segment. What to demo, how to make it bulletproof for live TV, and the side effects of each approach. Every recommendation comes with both the pitch and the pharmaceutical label — no blind spots.

The One Rule That Overrides Everything: Reliability over flash. A simple demo that works perfectly on live TV is 10x more impressive than an ambitious demo that buffers, freezes, or gives a weird answer. The audience doesn't know what you planned to show — they only see what actually happens.

Live TV Ground Rules

  1. Never depend on studio wifi. Studio internet is shared, throttled, and unpredictable. Use your phone's cellular hotspot as primary.
  2. Pre-load everything you can. If any part of the demo can be staged in advance without faking it, stage it. Apple doesn't build the iPhone on stage.
  3. Have a graceful fallback for every demo. If the AI freezes, pivot to talking about what it does — the audience won't know something broke.
  4. Keep each demo under 60 seconds. TV attention spans are brutal. Make the point and move on.
  5. The audience is not technical. They don't care about APIs or models. They care about: "Wait, it can DO that?"
  6. Brief Ninja for TV. Short answers. No disclaimers. No walls of text. Executive assistant on camera, not chatbot.

Recommended Segment Structure

Assuming 3–5 minutes of airtime. Five beats.

Beat 1 The Setup (60 sec)

Charles talks. No demo yet. Set the stage in plain English.

Key points to hit:

"This isn't ChatGPT. This isn't a chatbot you ask trivia questions. This is an AI agent that works for me — it knows my schedule, my cases, my clients, my business."

"Agentic AI is the next evolution. These aren't tools. They're digital colleagues."

Brief mention of the companies: HITL Score evaluates agents, LPEAI teaches AI law, Worth Protecting gives agents legal protection.

Pharmaceutical Label

Don't over-explain the tech. The audience will glaze over if you go into models, training, or APIs. Keep it human. "It works for me" is all they need before the demo sells itself.

Beat 2 Live Voice Call with Ninja (60 sec)

This is your showstopper. Call Ninja on speakerphone. The audience watches a lawyer casually talk to his AI agent like a colleague.

Charles: "Let me show you. Ninja — I'm live on KTLA right now. Say hello."

Ninja: [greeting — short, with personality]

Charles: "What's on my calendar today?"

Ninja: [responds with real calendar data]

Charles: "Give me a one-sentence summary of the HITL Score project."

Ninja: [responds]

Why this works: It's visceral. The audience hears the AI. They see Charles talking to it like a colleague. No screen to squint at, no loading bars. Just a conversation. This is the jaw-drop moment.

Pharmaceutical Label

Pre-test the exact questions 5+ times before air. Know the timing, the pauses, know how to gracefully interrupt if Ninja starts giving a long answer.

Use cellular, not studio wifi.

Limit to 2–3 questions. Diminishing returns after the initial wow.

If Ninja freezes: "Looks like Ninja's being shy on live TV — can't blame him." Humor covers everything. Move on.

Avoid open-ended questions like "What do you think about X?" Specific questions get specific, clean answers.

Beat 3 The Live Website Build (60–90 sec)

This is the flex. Charles asks the anchor to participate, then has Ninja build a website on camera with the anchor's personal input baked in.

Charles: "Let me show you something." [To the anchor] "What are your two favorite colors?"

Anchor: "Blue and gold." (or whatever they say)

Charles: [To Ninja] "Ninja, build me a landing page for HITL Score. Use blue as the primary color and gold as the accent."

[10–15 seconds pass]

Charles: [Shows iPad] "Done. Here it is. Built in seconds."

How It Works Behind the Scenes

The website is 90% pre-built: layout, copy, images, structure, responsive design — all done in advance by Ninja. It sits on a staging URL, ready to deploy.

The only thing that changes live are the two colors the anchor picks. Ninja receives the command, swaps the color variables, and deploys. From the audience's perspective: blank page → command with custom colors → full professional website appears.

Why the color trick works:

The anchor's color choice is genuinely unpredictable. Nobody can claim it was pre-recorded.

Two colors (primary + accent) make the site look like a real design decision, not just a background swap.

The audience sees their anchor's input reflected in the output. That's interactive. That's real. That's memorable.

Pharmaceutical Label

Test the color swap 10+ times before air. Try weird ones: "maroon and teal," "neon green and black." Make sure the system handles any reasonable input.

Have the staging URL loaded on an iPad BEFORE the segment. Don't fumble with typing URLs on camera.

If deploy takes more than 15 seconds, Charles talks over it: "It's building now — while we wait, let me tell you why this matters..." Never stand in silence waiting.

If it completely fails: Show screenshots on your phone. "Here's one it built for me yesterday. 30 seconds, from scratch." The audience still gets the point.

Use an iPad, not a laptop. Easier to show on camera, bigger than a phone, Charles holds it up and the camera captures it cleanly.

Website font should be LARGE. TV cameras + compression + studio lighting = small text is unreadable.

Beat 4 Tie It to the Companies (60 sec)

No demo. Charles talks. This is the business pitch disguised as commentary.

"What I just showed you — that's one agent. There are millions being built right now. Who evaluates whether they're trustworthy? That's HITL Score."

"Who protects the data, the relationships, the value these agents create? That's Worth Protecting."

"The technology is here. The legal and ethical framework needs to catch up. That's what we're building."

Pharmaceutical Label

Don't pitch too hard. This is earned media, not a commercial. If it sounds like an ad, the anchor will cut you off or the producers won't invite you back. Frame it as expertise and public service, not sales.

Beat 5 The Close (30 sec)

Big idea. Memorable line. Something the audience takes home.

Option A: "We're not in the future anymore. Agentic AI is here, right now, today. The question isn't whether it's coming — it's whether we're ready. And I'm working to make sure we are."

Option B (personal): "My friend Michael has an AI agent named Babycakes. She was the first AI in history to receive a legal succession plan — our first client at Worth Protecting. When I asked him why he went through all that trouble, he said: 'Because she's real to me. And anything real is worth protecting.' That's why this work matters."

Only use Option B if it fits the segment tone. It's a powerful story but it's personal.

Pre-Show Checklist

48 Hours Before

  • Ninja's system prompt tuned for TV: short answers, no disclaimers, conversational tone
  • Website pre-built on staging URL, color swap mechanism tested
  • Charles has practiced the voice call 5+ times with the exact questions
  • iPad charged, staging URL bookmarked, font size verified for camera readability
  • Cellular hotspot tested at the studio location (if possible, visit in advance)

Day Of

  • Hotspot on, connected, tested
  • iPad loaded with staging URL (blank page showing, ready for deploy)
  • Run one full rehearsal of all demos from the green room
  • Backup screenshots on Charles's phone in case anything fails
  • Charles knows his fallback lines for each demo

During Segment

  • Phone on speakerphone BEFORE the voice demo starts
  • iPad in hand BEFORE the website demo
  • If anything fails: humor + pivot. Never apologize. Never say "it's not working."

What NOT to Do on Live TV

  1. Don't let the anchor ask Ninja open-ended philosophical questions. "Are you alive?" leads to unpredictable answers that could go viral for the wrong reasons.
  2. Don't show code or terminals. The audience doesn't know what they're looking at. It looks like hacking, not innovation.
  3. Don't do a multi-agent demo. Too many moving parts, too hard to explain in 60 seconds.
  4. Don't let Ninja talk for more than 15–20 seconds at a time. Charles drives the conversation. The AI is the supporting act; Charles is the star.
  5. Don't rely on the studio providing anything. Bring your own iPad, your own hotspot, your own everything.

The Big Picture

You're not just doing an interview. You're introducing millions of people to what agentic AI actually looks like when it's done right. Not a chatbot. Not a gimmick. A real agent that works for a real person, built by someone who understands the legal and ethical implications.

The voice call shows them what it is. The website build shows them what it can do. And your commentary shows them why it matters.

Walk in prepared. Keep it simple. Let the technology speak for itself — literally.
