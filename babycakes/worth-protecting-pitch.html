The Shift

From CA‑only attorney product to national platform

Your original UPL flag on the first intake package is what forced the better model. You were right: a CA-licensed firm reviewing individual customer matters across 50 states was an unauthorized-practice-of-law landmine. So we moved the work across the regulatory line.

The $99 base product is now a pure software scrivener — customer fills their own intake, software generates a RUFADAA §872 standalone directive from their answers, no attorney touches individual files. Same legal theory LegalZoom proved across 50 states (NC 2016 statute, SC court's "scrivener" ruling). You blessed this via text on April 19.

The attorney tier is where you live — California clients who want a real bar-licensed review, hourly or flat-fee, attorney-client privilege, zero exposure on Tier 1. Phase 3 opens other states via bar-licensed franchise partners under the WP Seal. You never touch a Tier 1 file; we never touch a Tier 2 customer without you or a state partner.

The through-line: your UPL concern became the architectural backbone. Every decision below follows from honoring it fully.

Trust Architecture

The Subservience Rule

Named after the Megan Fox film — the robot who told the wife her husband had been unfaithful. That's exactly what we never build.

WP systems never expose customer agent-relationships

Default-private across every feature. No public queries, no meta-info leaks, no customer directories, no queryable verification endpoints unless the owner explicitly opts in per-artifact.

Server-held keys with audit discipline. We hold WP Vault passwords so we can release them to verified custodians. We do NOT hold third-party platform credentials (Replika, Character.AI, ChatGPT, etc.) — those live in the customer's own password manager; the directive instructs the custodian how to claim them via the platform's legacy-access process.

Franchisees inherit this rule contractually. State partners are bound by the same custodian standard or they lose the Seal. Subservience is not policy — it's a licensing condition.
